## Reviewer 1 (GeoAI / Urban Computing)

### Summary
This paper introduces SoundScape-ControlNet, a latent diffusion framework that conditions Stable Diffusion v2.1 on ISO 12913 perceptual soundscape descriptors (pleasantness, eventfulness, LAeq) concatenated with CLAP audio embeddings, alongside a ControlNet zero-conv spatial adapter fed by OSM-derived rasters. The framework is trained on 15,240 Mapillary images across NYC, London, and Singapore, evaluated with a four-arm ablation, and further analyzed via OLS/GWR/MGWR spatial regression on per-cell LPIPS. The central novelty claim — that structured ISO 12913 descriptors add conditioning value beyond raw CLAP embeddings — is directly confirmed by the ablation (Arm C vs. Arm D, FID 26.7 vs. 24.1), and the zero-shot Singapore transfer demonstrates practical cross-city generalizability.

### Major Issues

**M1.1 — FID comparability (Section 4.1, Table 2; Abstract):** The paper reports FID 24.1 on held-out Mapillary images and directly juxtaposes this with Sound2Scene's FID 28.3 on the VEGAS benchmark. The Discussion (Section 5.1) acknowledges this is not a controlled comparison, but the Abstract states "SoundScape-ControlNet achieves FID 24.1 versus 34.8 for a text-only Stable Diffusion baseline" and Table 2 includes Sound2Scene's FID in the same row alongside the authors' own arms. This placement invites readers to interpret the absolute FID values as directly comparable across different reference sets, resolution regimes, and training domains. The limitation is present but insufficiently foregrounded. The paper must either (a) remove the Sound2Scene FID from Table 2 and relegate it to a clearly labeled external reference in a separate comparative table with an explicit caveat, or (b) conduct at minimum a within-protocol replication by applying the Sound2Scene evaluation protocol on a shared subset of the Mapillary test set to confirm the directional ordering. The current note at the foot of Table 2 mentioning bootstrap CIs does not address the cross-benchmark comparability issue at all.

**M1.2 — Architecture differentiation from Uni-ControlNet (Section 3.3, Section 2.2):** The dual-path gating mechanism (Equation in Section 3.3.3) is formally identical to the Uni-ControlNet global-control pathway described in Zhao et al. (2023) and cited directly in Section 2.2. The novelty of the contribution rests on the acoustic modality being injected, not on the fusion mechanism itself — yet the paper's contributions list (Section 1.3, Contribution 1) presents the "dual-path ControlNet" as a new architectural construct without clearly stating that the gating design is adapted from Uni-ControlNet. This risks appearing as an overclaim of architectural novelty. The paper should be explicit that the fusion architecture is adapted from Uni-ControlNet, that the contribution is the novel acoustic conditioning pathway and ISO 12913 integration, and not a new fusion operator.

**M1.3 — CLAP vs. ImageBind encoder comparison missing from reported results (Section 3.3.1; Section 3.4, Arm E):** The paper defines Arm E as an ImageBind-substitution arm in the ablation design (Section 3.4) and states that CLAP was selected based on a "pilot sensitivity analysis (H5)" with "lower per-cell LPIPS in all three cities." However, Arm E results are entirely absent from Table 2, Table 3, and all subsequent reporting. The paper therefore makes an encoder choice claim that is supported only by an unreported pilot with no metrics, confidence intervals, or significance tests. Either the Arm E results must be reported in full as a final ablation arm with quantitative metrics (which is the principled approach) or the encoder selection rationale must be disclosed as a design decision with the pilot results in supplementary material with the exact LPIPS values and cities reported. As written, the CLAP superiority claim is unverifiable.

**M1.4 — Training data size and dataset coverage underdetermined (Section 3.2.1; Abstract):** The abstract states 15,240 Mapillary images across three cities, and Section 3.4 states 500 held-out locations per city. Section 3.2.1 specifies a minimum of 5,000 images per city and a minimum of three images per 500 m cell but does not state the actual final training set size per city after all exclusions. The per-city train/validation/test split is unspecified. For reproducibility and for interpreting why FID is "stable" above 500 images per group, the exact training, validation, and held-out image counts per city and per land-use class must be stated in a data summary table (which Table 2 in the methodology section is referenced as but whose contents are described via a cross-reference to "Table 2" — a numbering conflict with Table 2 in the results section).

### Minor Issues

- Section 3.3.3: The gating mechanism activates sigmoid(g_l) and (1 − sigmoid(g_l)) for local and global paths respectively. At initialization (g_l = 0), this gives equal weighting (0.5/0.5). The paper does not report the learned gate values at convergence per city or per resolution level. These values would be informative for understanding which path dominates at which scale and for reproducibility.
- Section 3.4: The Bonferroni correction is applied to "six simultaneous comparisons (3 cities × 2 arm pairs)." It is not stated which two arm pairs are tested. Presumably Arm D vs. Arm C and Arm D vs. Arm A, but this should be explicit.
- Section 4.2: Figure 4 is referenced twice (generation grid in Section 4.1 and LPIPS choropleth maps in Section 4.2). These appear to be two different figures. A numbering error appears to be present.
- Section 2.2: DiffPlace is cited as Wang et al. (2026), which is a future date relative to the paper's submission. This should be flagged as "in press" or "preprint" with a DOI or arXiv identifier.
- Section 4.3: The abstract reports "~2,400 regression observations" pooled but Table 3 suggests per-city MGWR sample sizes of 2,489 / 2,412 / 1,844 (total 6,745 after subsampling). The abstract's aggregate figure is misleading and should be corrected to per-city sample sizes.
- The paper does not report training time or inference time per image. For ISPRS readers interested in operational deployment, these figures are relevant.

### Recommendation: Major Revision

The core science is sound and the paper addresses a genuine gap. Issues M1.1 (FID comparability), M1.2 (architecture overclaim), and M1.3 (missing Arm E results) are all addressable with additional reporting or textual correction and do not require new experiments. M1.4 requires a data summary table. These revisions would substantially strengthen the paper's claim to methodological rigor.

## Reviewer 2 (Spatial Statistics / GIScience)

### Summary
This paper applies OLS, GWR, and MGWR to per-cell median LPIPS scores as a spatially varying dependent variable, using acoustic and morphological covariates across three UTM-projected city grids. The methodology section is unusually careful about spatial analysis conventions: CRS is stated for all datasets and operations, distance computations are explicitly performed in projected UTM coordinates, MGWR sample limits are enforced with stratified hexagonal spatial subsampling, and the spatial cross-validation design explicitly blocks at a radius of at least 3x the GWR bandwidth. These are above-average standards for a GeoAI-type paper. Several concerns remain that affect the validity and interpretability of the spatial regression results.

### Major Issues

**M2.1 — Validity of per-cell median LPIPS as a spatial dependent variable (Sections 3.5.1, 3.2.1):** Per-cell median LPIPS is constructed from a minimum of three Mapillary images per cell, where each LPIPS score is the perceptual similarity between a generated image and a reference image at the same location. The fundamental problem is that LPIPS is a function of both the model output and the reference image, and the reference images themselves are spatially autocorrelated in quality and content (denser coverage in commercial zones, sparser in periphery; Section 5.3 acknowledges Mapillary density bias). The paper does not decompose whether spatial variation in per-cell median LPIPS is driven by (a) spatially varying generation quality, (b) spatially varying reference image quality (different cameras, weather, occlusion, age), or (c) spatially varying image density biasing the cell median. Without this decomposition, the MGWR coefficients represent a composite that conflates generation model performance with data collection artifacts. At minimum, a sensitivity analysis regressing per-cell LPIPS against image count, image age variance, and camera type per cell — and reporting whether the main MGWR coefficients are robust to inclusion of these controls — is necessary to establish that the dependent variable measures what is claimed.

**M2.2 — Multi-city pooled OLS covariate construction and cross-city comparability (Section 3.5.4):** The paper proposes a "pooled OLS model with city dummy variables (London as reference category)" in Section 3.5.4 but this model is described only in passing as a "supplementary result." The main spatial analysis runs GWR and MGWR only on per-city projected datasets. This is correct for GWR/MGWR (running them on mixed UTM coordinates across cities would be invalid), but the paper does not adequately address whether the per-city MGWR results are quantitatively comparable across cities. LAeq is kriged from different data sources in different cities (Noise-Planet alone for NYC and Singapore, Noise-Planet plus BN-LOUD for London). This asymmetry means the acoustic covariate has different measurement error structures in London versus the other two cities. No sensitivity analysis tests whether the London MGWR results differ substantively when estimated using Noise-Planet data alone (matching the NYC and Singapore protocol) versus the combined BN-LOUD + Noise-Planet surface.

**M2.3 — MGWR bandwidth confidence intervals: method not specified (Section 3.5.4, Table 4):** Table 4 reports "Bootstrap 95% CI on bandwidth computed via 2,000 resamples on MGWR backfitting output." MGWR bootstrap bandwidth inference is not a standardized operation in the mgwr Python package: the package provides standard errors on coefficients but not on bandwidths via bootstrap. The paper does not specify whether these CIs are obtained by (a) bootstrapping the full dataset and re-running MGWR bandwidth selection on each resample, (b) parametric approximation of the AICc surface near the optimal bandwidth, or (c) a custom resampling scheme applied to the backfitting output. The claim that the acoustic pleasantness and building density bandwidth CIs do not overlap (and therefore represent "statistically distinguishable scales at p < 0.05") is the most important methodological claim in the spatial analysis section; it requires an explicit, reproducible description of the inference procedure. If CIs are computed by simply bootstrapping the dataset and re-running full MGWR on each resample (the most defensible approach), this should be stated, the computational cost noted, and the number of resamples confirmed as 2,000.

**M2.4 — Spatial error model (SEM) comparison under-reported (Section 4.3):** Table 3 omits SEM results entirely. The footnote to Table 3 states "SEM adjusted R²: NYC 0.49, London 0.54, Singapore 0.57" but does not report SEM AICc, SEM residual Moran's I, or SEM block CV RMSE. The role of the SEM in the paper's argument is to confirm that GWR/MGWR gains reflect spatial non-stationarity rather than residual autocorrelation, which is a critical interpretive point. Without the SEM's residual Moran's I, it is impossible to determine whether SEM eliminates autocorrelation as effectively as MGWR, and the paper's argument for non-stationarity over autocorrelation is weakened. The SEM should be included as a full row in Table 3 with all four fit statistics.

### Minor Issues

- Section 3.5.3: The spatial weights matrix uses k = 8 KNN built on 500 m UTM centroids. For a regular 500 m grid with no gaps, k = 8 is exactly the queen contiguity set, making it equivalent to a queen contiguity matrix. This equivalence should be noted and a robustness check with rook contiguity (k = 4) is appropriate alongside the already-mentioned k = 4 and k = 12 sensitivity checks.
- Section 3.5.4: The MGWR model specification uses a Gaussian kernel (stated in the kernel equation), but the GWR model uses "adaptive bisquare kernel" (stated in Section 3.5.4). It is standard practice to use the same kernel family for both GWR and MGWR to ensure that AICc values are comparable. The paper should clarify whether MGWR also uses bisquare or Gaussian, and if they differ, address whether the AICc comparison between GWR and MGWR is kernel-confounded.
- Section 3.5.2: VIF > 10 is used as the collinearity threshold for covariate exclusion, which is appropriate. However, the paper does not report the actual VIF values or the final retained covariate set per city. These should appear in a supplementary table given that pleasantness and LAeq may be correlated (the paper mentions |r| = 0.70 as the eventfulness-dropping criterion but does not report the observed pairwise correlations).
- Section 4.3: MGWR sample sizes (NYC 2,489; London 2,412; Singapore 1,844) are below the 3,000 limit. But the H3 resolution 8 hexagonal subsampling is described in Section 3.5.4 as targeting n ≤ 2,500 for NYC and London explicitly (max_n_mgwr: 2500 in the config YAML). The discrepancy between YAML (2,500) and methodology text (3,000 stated as the general MGWR limit) should be resolved for clarity.
- The Moran's I correlogram (supplementary Figure S1) is referenced in Section 4.2 as showing autocorrelation decay to near-zero at approximately 3–5 km. The GWR optimal bandwidth for NYC (k = 58 neighbors at 500 m spacing) corresponds to approximately 29 km effective radius — considerably larger than the 4–5 km autocorrelation range. This apparent scale mismatch between the spatial dependence range and the optimal GWR bandwidth should be interpreted explicitly, as it reflects the fact that GWR bandwidth is determined by covariate-response relationships, not autocorrelation range alone.

### Recommendation: Major Revision

The spatial analysis framework is well-designed and the paper applies best-practice geo-statistical conventions far more rigorously than typical GeoAI papers. However, issues M2.1 (LPIPS as a confounded DV), M2.3 (MGWR bandwidth CI method not specified), and M2.4 (SEM incomplete) together undermine the strength of the paper's central spatial claims. M2.2 (cross-city acoustic data asymmetry) requires a sensitivity test. All four issues are addressable without new data collection.

## Reviewer 3 (Urban Soundscape / Environmental Acoustics)

### Summary
This paper claims to be the first framework that uses ISO 12913 perceptual soundscape descriptors — specifically pleasantness, eventfulness, and A-weighted SPL — as conditioning signals for generative image synthesis. The framing correctly identifies the paradigm inversion: rather than treating soundscape quality as a dependent variable, the paper uses it as a generative prior. The framework's ability to improve FID and LPIPS when ISO 12913 conditioning is added (Arm C to Arm D) provides the primary empirical evidence for this inversion being useful.

### Major Issues

**M3.1 — ISO 12913 descriptors are modeled, not measured (Sections 3.2.4, 3.3.1; Abstract):** The abstract and contributions list present ISO 12913 pleasantness and eventfulness as conditioning inputs, but these values are never directly measured for the SoundingEarth or Noise-Planet acoustic data. The actual pipeline is: (1) train a 3-layer MLP on the ARAUS dataset to predict pleasantness and eventfulness from CLAP embeddings; (2) run this MLP at inference on SoundingEarth audio clips to produce predicted ISO 12913 scores; (3) use these predicted scores as conditioning inputs. This means the framework conditions on model-predicted approximations of ISO 12913 descriptors, not on perceptual measurements. The paper does not report the ARAUS-trained MLP's prediction accuracy on a held-out ARAUS subset (e.g., Pearson r, RMSE for pleasantness and eventfulness), nor does it report any validation that the MLP's predictions on SoundingEarth audio are calibrated (since SoundingEarth field recordings are a different acoustic domain from ARAUS's controlled augmented soundscapes). The precision of the ISO 12913 conditioning signal is therefore unknown. If the MLP has, say, r = 0.60 for pleasantness on ARAUS, the "structured perceptual prior" is substantially noisier than the framing implies. The MLP's in-distribution and out-of-distribution validation must be reported.

**M3.2 — Noise-Planet crowdsourced data bias not adequately addressed (Section 3.2.2; Section 5.3):** The paper acknowledges in Section 5.3 that Noise-Planet data "exhibits a density bias toward central urban areas" and that "kriging interpolation errors in these areas are larger." However, the Noise-Planet and NoiseCapture datasets have a second, less-discussed problem: systematic sampling bias by time-of-day and user behavior. Urban quiet-seeking walkers (who use NoiseCapture recreationally) over-sample parks, pedestrian zones, and weekend mornings; commuters contribute less data; industrial zones and night-time environments are under-sampled by design. This is not a simple spatial density issue but a selective behavioral bias in the acoustic measurement process. The paper needs to (a) report the time-of-day distribution of Noise-Planet measurements per city to characterize the bias, and (b) assess whether this bias systematically affects the LAeq covariate in the spatial regression, particularly given that time-of-day has a quasi-global MGWR bandwidth (Section 4.3, Table 4), which might partly reflect the lack of localized time-of-day signal in the biased Noise-Planet data rather than a genuinely uniform diurnal effect.

**M3.3 — Ecological validity of soundscape conditioning for visual generation (Sections 3.3.1, 5.1):** The core causal assumption in Section 5.1 — that "a high-pleasantness soundscape signal carries an implicit scene-category prior (residential or park)" — is drawn from Kang et al. (2018) and Qin et al. (2019), both of which established acoustic-visual statistical co-variation. But co-variation in measurement does not validate conditioning: the paper uses a pleasantness score predicted from audio to condition a visual generator, and the performance gain is demonstrated empirically (FID improvement). What is absent is any test that the generated images, when rated by human evaluators for ISO 12913 pleasantness, actually produce higher-pleasantness images when conditioned on high-pleasantness acoustic inputs and lower-pleasantness images when conditioned on low-pleasantness inputs. Without this perceptual validation loop — even a small pilot with 50 generated image pairs rated by 10 evaluators using the SATP standardized scale (Aletta et al., 2023) — the ecological validity of the framework remains unverified. FID and LPIPS measure distributional and perceptual similarity to reference images; they do not confirm that the acoustic-visual semantic coupling is actually encoded in the generated images. A human evaluation study is necessary to support the paper's claims about "acoustically coherent" output.

**M3.4 — ISO 12913-2 and -3 not cited (Section 2.3):** The paper cites ISO 12913-1 (2014) as the foundational standard but does not cite ISO 12913-2 (2018), which specifies the data collection method for soundscape assessments, or ISO 12913-3 (2019), which specifies the framework for data analysis. ISO 12913-2 is directly relevant because the paper uses ISO 12913 perceptual descriptors as conditioning variables — the legitimacy of those variables depends on the measurement method defined in Part 2. The absence of these citations is a substantive gap in the soundscape methodology framing, not a minor omission.

### Minor Issues

- Section 2.3: The ARAUS dataset is from Ooi et al. (2022), but the paper should also cite Watcharasupat et al. (2021), the DCASE Task 9 organizers, who provide the formal evaluation protocol for soundscape attribute prediction on which Watcharasupat et al. (2022a) and (2022b) are based.
- Section 5.1: The statement that "calm, low-eventfulness soundscapes predict greener, lower-density visual environments" should be cited to Kang et al. (2018) and Qin et al. (2019) more precisely, identifying the specific finding (r = 0.52–0.74 for residential and park zones) rather than paraphrasing loosely.
- Section 2.3: Tarlao et al. (2024) is cited for structural equation modeling of appropriateness, which is a relevant methodological precedent. However, appropriateness is a secondary ISO 12913 dimension, and the paper does not include it as a conditioning variable. This should be either included (at least as a future work direction) or the citation should be more precisely scoped.
- Section 3.2.2: The ARAUS transfer to SoundingEarth clips involves domain shift (controlled augmented soundscapes vs. field recordings). The paper should state whether any domain adaptation was applied to the MLP or whether it is used as-is.
- The paper uses LAeq (time-averaged equivalent sound level) as one ISO 12913 scalar, but ISO 12913 does not specify LAeq as a primary attribute — it specifies pleasantness and eventfulness as the two orthogonal dimensions. LAeq is a physical acoustic descriptor that correlates with ISO 12913 attributes but is not part of the standard itself. The framing should clarify that LAeq is included as a physical correlate, not as an ISO 12913 perceptual attribute, to avoid misrepresenting the standard.

### Recommendation: Major Revision

The paper's soundscape framing is sophisticated and the inversion of ISO 12913 from measurement endpoint to generative prior is a genuinely novel conceptual contribution. However, issues M3.1 (unvalidated MLP accuracy), M3.2 (Noise-Planet temporal bias not characterized), and M3.3 (absence of perceptual validation of generated images) together mean the ecological validity of the central claim remains unestablished. M3.4 (missing ISO 12913-2 and -3 citations) is a substantive framing gap. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the paper's credibility in the soundscape science community.

## Overall Decision

**Consensus recommendation: Major Revision**

All three reviewers independently recommend Major Revision. The paper addresses a clearly identified and genuinely novel gap — the intersection of ISO 12913 soundscape perception, GeoAI generative synthesis, and spatial regression analysis — and the spatial methodology section is markedly more rigorous than typical GeoAI papers in its handling of CRS, projections, MGWR sample limits, and spatial CV design. The ablation results are internally consistent and the stepwise FID improvement narrative is persuasive. However, six issues classified as Major across the three reviews require substantive revision before the paper can be recommended for acceptance.

## Path to Acceptance

The following items must be addressed in a revised manuscript. Items 1–3 are the highest priority and require either new analysis or substantive rewriting.

1. **Report Arm E (CLAP vs. ImageBind) results in full** (Reviewer 1, M1.3). Add Arm E as a table row in Table 2 with FID, SSIM, mIoU, and per-city LPIPS, accompanied by Wilcoxon tests versus Arm D. Remove the vague "pilot analysis (H5)" justification for the CLAP selection. If full Arm E training is not feasible, report the pilot results quantitatively in supplementary material.

2. **Validate the ISO 12913 MLP prediction head** (Reviewer 3, M3.1). Report held-out ARAUS prediction accuracy (Pearson r, RMSE) for pleasantness and eventfulness. Report or describe a calibration check for MLP predictions on SoundingEarth clips. Reframe the abstract and contributions to reflect that the conditioning signal is a model-predicted approximation of ISO 12913 scores, not directly measured perceptual ratings.

3. **Specify and justify MGWR bootstrap bandwidth CI computation method** (Reviewer 2, M2.3). Provide an explicit description of the bootstrap procedure used to compute bandwidth confidence intervals. Confirm that the non-overlapping CIs for acoustic pleasantness vs. building density are computed via full MGWR re-runs on 2,000 dataset resamples (not a parametric approximation). Add this to the supplementary methods.

4. **Move Sound2Scene FID comparison out of Table 2** (Reviewer 1, M1.1). Create a separate comparative reference table (or a clearly labeled extension to the discussion table) with explicit notation that FID values across different benchmarks are not directly comparable. Update the abstract to remove the juxtaposed numbers if the comparison cannot be placed on controlled footing.

5. **Clarify dual-path architecture novelty vs. Uni-ControlNet** (Reviewer 1, M1.2). In the contributions list and abstract, state clearly that the fusion gating mechanism is adapted from Uni-ControlNet (Zhao et al., 2023) and that the contribution is the acoustic conditioning pathway and ISO 12913 integration. Remove any language suggesting a new fusion operator.

6. **Add SEM to Table 3 with full statistics** (Reviewer 2, M2.4). Include SEM (PySAL ML_Error) as a full row in Table 3 with adjusted R², AICc, residual Moran's I, and block CV RMSE per city to allow proper comparison of non-stationarity versus autocorrelation sources.

7. **Report LAeq acoustic data asymmetry sensitivity analysis** (Reviewer 2, M2.2). Add a supplementary table testing whether London MGWR results change materially when Noise-Planet-only LAeq (rather than BN-LOUD + Noise-Planet) is used as the acoustic covariate, enabling cross-city comparability of the acoustic DV.

8. **Characterize Noise-Planet time-of-day sampling bias** (Reviewer 3, M3.2). Add a supplementary table or paragraph describing the time-of-day distribution of Noise-Planet measurements per city and whether this bias could explain the quasi-global MGWR bandwidth for the time-of-day covariate.

9. **Conduct or cite a perceptual validation of generated images** (Reviewer 3, M3.3). Either present a pilot human evaluation study (minimum 50 generated image pairs rated on pleasantness using the SATP scale) or explicitly flag the absence of this validation as a clear limitation in Section 5.3, noting that FID/LPIPS confirm distributional fidelity but not ISO 12913 semantic coherence in generated outputs.

10. **Cite ISO 12913-2 (2018) and ISO 12913-3 (2019)** (Reviewer 3, M3.4) in Section 2.3 and wherever ISO 12913 perceptual descriptors are described as conditioning variables.

11. **Add data summary table with per-city image counts** (Reviewer 1, M1.4). Provide a table listing training, validation, and held-out test image counts per city and per OSM land-use class, before and after temporal exclusions.

12. **Resolve Figure 4 numbering conflict** (Reviewer 1, minor). Section 4.1 references Figure 4 for the generation grid and Section 4.2 references Figure 4 again for LPIPS choropleth maps. These must be distinct figure numbers.

`Overall Score: 6.8 (N:8.0, R:6.5, L:7.5, C:7.5, I:8.0)`

**Score dimension notes:**
- **Novelty (N: 8.0):** Genuinely novel intersection of ISO 12913, latent diffusion, and MGWR-on-LPIPS. The paradigm inversion treating soundscape descriptors as generative priors rather than endpoints is conceptually strong. Slight deduction for the architectural novelty overstatement (Uni-ControlNet gating).
- **Rigor (R: 6.5):** The spatial analysis design is unusually careful (CRS handling, spatial CV, MGWR subsampling). Deductions for: unvalidated MGWR bandwidth CI method, missing Arm E results, unvalidated ISO 12913 MLP, confounded LPIPS DV, incomplete SEM comparison, and cross-city acoustic data asymmetry. These are significant methodological gaps that cannot be resolved by editorial judgment.
- **Literature coverage (L: 7.5):** Strong coverage of GeoAI synthesis (Mai et al., 2023), ControlNet variants, ARAUS, Fan et al. (2020), Shen et al. (2022), Fotheringham et al. (2017). Missing ISO 12913-2 and -3 and the DCASE Task 9 papers. Synthesis table is well-constructed.
- **Clarity (C: 7.5):** Active voice, specific numbers throughout, research question links maintained across sections. Figure numbering errors and the abstract's misleading "~2,400 regression observations" reduce clarity. The architecture description requires clarification on Uni-ControlNet provenance.
- **Impact (I: 8.0):** Practical applications (gap-filling, intervention visualization, privacy-preserving mapping) are concrete and well-grounded. The zero-shot Singapore result at FID 27.3 < 30 is a strong practical demonstration. The Global South extension direction is well-motivated. Deduction for absent perceptual validation, which is the link between the technical results and the claimed impact for urban planners.
